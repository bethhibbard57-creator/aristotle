Aristotle · Μετὰ τὰ Φυσικά — Secondary Aporia Moments

Individual aporiai embedded in Gamma, Delta, Epsilon, Theta, Lambda, and Mu-Nu · their original sources · their resolution · their connection to the thesis

What these are: Beyond the three sustained aporia books (Beta, Zeta, Iota) and the recapitulation (Kappa), the Metaphysics contains individual aporiai embedded within otherwise continuous arguments. These are not book-length sustained investigations but precise local difficulties — moments where the argument pauses to acknowledge a genuine difficulty before resolving it and moving on. Each is philosophically significant. Collectively they show that the aporia method is not confined to the formally designated aporia books but runs through the entire Metaphysics as the standard mode of philosophical inquiry: pose the difficulty honestly, examine both sides, resolve by identifying the suppressed qualification.

Γ — Gamma: Being qua being · the defence of LNC and PEM

Γ.2 1003b22–1004a9
Aporia: if being is said in many ways, is there one science of being or many?
The difficulty
Being is said in as many ways as there are categories — substance, quality, quantity, relation, and the rest. If being is thus multiple, how can there be one science of being? A single science has a single subject; if being has many senses, does first philosophy have many subjects?
Resolution
The pros hen (toward-one) relation: all the senses of being are related to a single primary sense — substance. As 'healthy' has many senses (the healthy diet, the healthy colour, the healthy person) all related to a single primary referent (health as a state of the body), so 'being' has many senses all related to the primary sense (substance). One science investigates being because all being is related to substance as its primary instance.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 251a–259d — the five greatest kinds and their interrelation; being as one of them but not reducible to any single kind
Parmenides — being as one; the monist challenge to the plurality of being
The pros hen relation is the ontological ground of the per se / accidental predication distinction at the level of the categories. All categories are said to be by reference to substance — which means the truth conditions for all predications are ultimately grounded in the categorical placement of the subject as a substance. The pros hen relation makes the entire Porphyrian tree a unified truth-condition structure rather than a collection of separate predication contexts.
Γ.4 1006a11–1007a20
Aporia: can LNC be demonstrated, and what happens if someone denies it?
The difficulty
LNC is the most certain of all principles. But can it be demonstrated? If it can, it is not a first principle — it has something prior from which it is demonstrated. If it cannot, how can we refute the person who denies it? The difficulty: first principles cannot be demonstrated without circularity, but the denier of a first principle seems to require demonstration to be convinced.
Resolution — the elenctic method
LNC cannot be demonstrated — it is the condition of demonstration, prior to all demonstration. But it can be defended elentically: the denier of LNC must say something — must use language — and as soon as they do, they presuppose that their words have a determinate meaning, which presupposes LNC. The denier refutes themselves in the act of denying. The elenctic defence does not demonstrate LNC; it shows that denial is self-undermining.
Original sources
Heraclitus — everything is and is not; the unity of opposites as a denial of LNC
Protagoras — man is the measure; what appears true to each is true; apparent contradiction is not genuine
Plato — Euthydemus 283e–286d — the Sophists Euthydemus and Dionysodorus exploit apparent contradictions; Plato's attempt to refute them
The elenctic defence of LNC is the model for the elenctic method of Topics VIII applied to the most fundamental logical principle. The same method that dissolves the Sophistic paradoxes (Sophistical Refutations) defends LNC against its most radical deniers. Priest's dialetheism is the sophisticated modern form of the Heraclitean denial — and the same elenctic response applies: the dialetheist uses language that presupposes LNC in the very act of asserting true contradictions.
Γ.6 1011b13–1012a17
Aporia: does PEM hold universally? The problem of the future contingent
The difficulty
PEM asserts that for every proposition, either it or its negation is true. But for future contingent propositions — 'there will be a sea battle tomorrow' — it seems that neither the proposition nor its negation is yet true or false. This threatens PEM for a whole class of propositions.
Resolution
PEM holds for the disjunction — either there will or will not be a sea battle, and this disjunction is necessarily true now. But neither disjunct is yet determined — the before-and-after structure of time has not yet resolved the contingency. PEM applies to the disjunction; it does not apply to each unresolved disjunct with equal force. The temporal qualification — same time — is the key. The disjunction is necessarily true now; neither disjunct is determined now.
Original sources
Plato — Theaetetus 157b–160e — the analysis of perception and the moment; what is true at one moment need not be true at another
Diodorus Cronus — the Master Argument: if something is possible and will not be, it was never possible. This makes all future contingents either necessary or impossible — and eliminates genuine contingency
This is the Gamma version of De Int. Ch. 9's Sea Battle. The resolution is the same: the temporal identity condition. PEM applies to what is temporally resolved — not to what is still genuinely contingent. The future contingent is the clearest case of a predication whose truth conditions are not yet fully determined — and which therefore cannot be subjected to LNC or PEM without suppressing the temporal qualification.

Δ — Delta: The philosophical lexicon · implicit aporiai in each entry

Δ.1 1012b34–1013a23
Aporia implicit in the six senses of arche: how can the starting point be anywhere?
The implicit aporia
Arche has six senses — the beginning of spatial extent, the best starting point for learning, the first material part, the external source of motion, the final cause as governing principle, and the logical starting point. If the starting point can be any of these six, how does inquiry have a determinate beginning? And why does Aristotle say the starting point can be anywhere?
The resolution
The starting point varies by context and by the epistemic position of the inquirer. What is first in nature (the formal-final cause, the Unmoved Mover) is not first for us — we begin from what is more knowable to us (the sensible, the particular) and work toward what is more knowable in itself (the universal, the necessary). The six senses of arche are not in competition — each is appropriate to a different level of the inquiry and a different epistemic starting point.
Original sources
Thales — water as the arche — the material starting point
Plato — Republic 510b–511d — the unhypothetical first principle as the true arche; the dialectician works both up and down from it
Pythagoreans — the One as the arche of number and hence of all things
The six senses of arche confirm that the philosophical argument can be entered from anywhere — the starting point is wherever you are. This is Topics Book VIII's principle applied to first philosophy: the best starting point for inquiry is the one most accessible to the inquirer. The corpus can be entered from the logical end (the Organon), the ontological end (the Metaphysics), the empirical end (the biological works), or the ethical end (the NE). All archai. All routes to the same terminus.
Δ.7 1017a7–b9
Aporia implicit in the senses of being: why is being not a genus?
The implicit aporia
Being is said in many ways — as the categories, as accidental being, as being as truth, as being in actuality and potentiality. If being is the most universal term, why is it not the highest genus? A genus is what is common to many; being is common to everything. Yet Aristotle denies that being is a genus.
The resolution
Being is not a genus because it cannot be differentiated by differentiae that are not themselves beings. Every differentia would have to be a being — and a genus cannot be differentiated by what already falls under it. The differentiae of a genus must be outside the genus. Since nothing is outside being, being has no differentiae and is therefore not a genus. It is a transcendental attribute — said of everything — not a genus within which things are classified.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 254b–e — being as one of the five greatest kinds; the question of whether being is a genus or something that pervades all genera
Parmenides — being as the one genus of all; monism as the consequence of treating being as a genus
Being is not a genus — this is the ontological ground of the claim that LNC applies only within a genus, not across all being universally. Contrariety (Iota.4) is the greatest difference within a genus; since being is not a genus, there is no contrary to being as such. The unlimited universality of being is why it cannot generate the per se predications that the Porphyrian tree requires — only at the level of specific genera do per se predications become possible.

Ε — Epsilon: Being qua being · accidental being · being as truth

Ε.2–3 1026b2–1027b16
Aporia: is there a science of accidental being?
The difficulty
Accidental being — what holds neither always nor for the most part but by coincidence (the musical builder, the pale man) — appears to be something rather than nothing. But there is no science of it — no demonstration of coincidences (Post. An. I.30). If it is real but unknowable, what is its ontological status?
Resolution
Accidental being is real but is not a proper subject of science. It exists in the way that what happens by chance exists — caused, but not by a determinate and repeatable causal chain that could be the subject of a universal demonstration. The causes of accidents are themselves accidents — indefinitely regressive and therefore beyond scientific grasp. Accidental being is the ontological floor below which first philosophy does not descend.
Original sources
Plato — Theaetetus 156c–160e — the flux doctrine: everything that happens by perception is a coincidence of the slow (the object) and the fast (the perceiver); Plato's account of what lies below stable being
Sophists generally — exploitation of accidental predications to generate apparent contradictions; the Sophist is the expert in accidental being
Accidental being as beyond science is the ontological ground of the per se / accidental distinction that runs through the entire logical apparatus. Every paradox that generates a truth glut does so by treating an accidental predication as if it were per se — making the coincidental appear necessary. The dissolution restores the qualification: what is true of this subject in these circumstances (accidental) is not true of this subject in virtue of what it is (per se). The Sophist exploits accidental being; the philosopher dissolves it.
Ε.4 1027b17–1028a6
Aporia: is being as truth in things or in the mind?
The difficulty
Being as truth — the being that belongs to true affirmations and the non-being that belongs to false ones — seems to be being of a special kind. But is it in things or in the mind? If in things, then falsehood would require non-being in things. If in the mind, then first philosophy, which investigates being in things, has nothing to say about truth.
Resolution
Being as truth is in the combining and separating — in the mind's act of affirmation and negation — not in things themselves. A stone does not have truth or falsity in it; the proposition 'the stone is white' has truth or falsity depending on the stone's actual colour. Being as truth is therefore not investigated by first philosophy (which investigates being in things) but by logic and psychology. This is why De Interpretatione, not the Metaphysics, is the proper place to analyse propositional truth.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 261c–264b — the analysis of false statement as combining what does not fit; falsehood requires the interweaving of forms, not an impossible contact with non-being
Plato — Theaetetus 187b–201c — the difficulty of false belief; how can the mind be in contact with what is not?
Being as truth in the mind — not in things — is the ontological ground of the distinction between the logical level (De Interpretatione, the elenchus conditions) and the ontological level (the Metaphysics, the analysis of substance). The logical paradoxes (the Liar, Russell's paradox) arise at the level of being as truth — in the combining and separating of the mind. Their dissolution is logical (the secundum quid move). The ontological ground of their dissolution is in the Metaphysics — in the analysis of how categorical placement and the per se / accidental distinction determine what truth values are possible for a given predication.

Θ — Theta: Actuality and potentiality

Θ.3–4 1046b29–1047b30
Aporia: the Megarian position — only what is actual is possible
The Megarian position
The Megarians (Diodorus Cronus, Stilpo) held that only what is actual is possible. A builder who is not building is not capable of building — the capacity only exists when it is exercised. This eliminates the distinction between potentiality and actuality and makes all unrealised possibilities simply impossible.
The difficulty this generates
If only what is actual is possible, then: a person who is sitting cannot stand (their capacity to stand is not actual while they are sitting); knowledge is destroyed when not exercised (the doctor who is not currently treating a patient has no medical knowledge); the cold is hot when it is being heated (the capacity of heat is not present until it is actual). All of these are absurd.
Resolution
Potentiality is real and distinct from actuality. The builder who is not building still has the capacity to build — a genuine potentiality that persists even when not exercised. The Megarian position generates the same error as the paradox of weakness of will (NE VII.3): it collapses the distinction between having a capacity and exercising it. Restoring the distinction: the builder has the capacity (potential) to build even when not building (actual).
Original sources
Diodorus Cronus — the Master Argument: nothing is possible except what is or will be actual; the possible is defined by what actually occurs
Stilpo — the elimination of predicates: only bare particulars are real; no universal predication is genuine
Plato — Sophist 247d–e — the materialists who hold that only what can be touched is real; the closest Platonic analogue to the Megarian position
The Megarian position is the ancient form of the claim that intuitionistic logic is correct — only what is actually constructible is possible, and PEM fails for unconstructed possibilities. Aristotle's response is the same as the response to intuitionism: potentiality is real, the distinction between having and using is real, and PEM applies to what is actually determined, not to what is still genuinely potential. The Megarian aporia and its resolution in Theta.3–4 is the ontological ground of the intuitionistic logic entry in the Russell erasure document.
Θ.8 1049b4–1051a3
Aporia: is actuality prior to potentiality? In what sense?
The difficulty
In one sense potentiality is prior to actuality — the seed is prior to the tree, the child prior to the adult, the matter prior to the form. In another sense actuality is prior — the actual tree is what the seed is for, the adult is what the child is becoming, the form is what the matter is potentially. Which priority is primary?
Resolution
Actuality is prior to potentiality in three senses: in definition (to define the potential requires reference to the actual — the potential builder is defined by reference to actual building); in time (something actually F precedes each individual potentially F — the actual man generates the potentially-man); in substance (what is eternal and primary is actual without potentiality — the Unmoved Mover). Potentiality is prior only in the temporal order of generation for individuals — not in definition, not in substance.
Original sources
Plato — Timaeus 27d–28a — being (always actual) prior to becoming (always in process of actualisation); Plato's implicit priority of actuality
Empedocles — the eternal Love and Strife as the actual principles prior to the perishable compounds they generate
Pythagoreans — the One as both prior to and the actuality that number expresses
The priority of actuality is the ontological ground of the claim that the truth conditions for per se predications are grounded in form — which is actuality — rather than in matter — which is potentiality. The per se attributes of a species are those that belong to the species in virtue of its form — its actuality. The accidental attributes are those that belong to the individual in virtue of its matter — its potentiality for variation. The priority of actuality over potentiality is the metaphysical foundation of the priority of per se predication over accidental predication.

Λ — Lambda: The cosmological argument · the Unmoved Mover

Λ.6 1071b3–1072a18
Aporia: how many unmoved movers are there? The problem of the number of spheres
The difficulty
The astronomical observations of Eudoxus and Callippus require many celestial spheres — 47 or 55. If each sphere requires an unmoved mover, there are 47 or 55 unmoved movers. But then there is no single first principle — first philosophy arrives at a plurality of divine minds rather than the unity it sought.
Resolution
There is one primary Unmoved Mover — the unmoved mover of the outermost sphere — and subsidiary unmoved movers for the inner spheres. The primary Unmoved Mover is the first principle; the others are secondary and dependent. The unity of first philosophy is preserved — there is one primary arche — while the multiplicity of astronomical observation is accommodated.
Original sources
Plato — Timaeus 38c–40d — the planets as divine visible gods, each with its own soul moving it; Plato's plurality of divine movers
Anaxagoras — Nous as the single principle of motion; but Anaxagoras left unclear how many things Nous moves and how
Pythagoreans — the Central Fire and the counter-earth as the cosmological first principles; multiplicity at the cosmological level
This aporia reveals the tension between first philosophy (which seeks one first principle) and astronomy (which requires many movers). Aristotle resolves it by a hierarchy — one primary, many secondary — that preserves both the unity of the first principle and the astronomical data. The resolution is not fully satisfying — the relation between the primary and secondary movers is not fully worked out. Lambda.8 is one of the most honestly incomplete passages in the corpus.
The tension between one primary principle and many secondary ones is the cosmological expression of the pros hen relation of Gamma.2. All the secondary movers are related to the primary Unmoved Mover as all the senses of being are related to substance. The hierarchy of movers mirrors the hierarchy of being — many instances of a single type of causation, all oriented toward and grounded in one primary instance. The tetralemmic structure appears here too: one (the primary mover) · many (the secondary movers) · the compound motion they produce · the silence of Lambda.9 about what lies beyond even the primary mover.
Λ.9 1074b15–1075a10
Aporia: what does the Unmoved Mover think? Can it think anything other than itself?
The difficulty
The Unmoved Mover is self-thinking thought — noēsis noēseōs. But what does it think? If it thinks something other than itself, it depends on that object for its thinking and is not fully self-sufficient. If it thinks only itself, what is the content of its thought? And how does its self-thinking thought exercise any causal efficacy over the cosmos?
The apophatic limit
Lambda.9 does not fully resolve this difficulty. It asserts that the Unmoved Mover's thought is the best thought and that it thinks itself — and then falls silent. The thinker and the thought are identical in the Unmoved Mover; the duality of knower and known is dissolved. But how this is possible, and what its content is, exceeds ordinary subject-predicate description. The text approaches the limit and does not go beyond it.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 509b — the Form of the Good is beyond being and essence; it cannot be fully described but only approached
Plato — Parmenides 142a — if the One is, it has being, but its being cannot be fully predicated without generating an infinite regress of predication
Plato — Seventh Letter 341c–d — the highest philosophical knowledge cannot be expressed in words; it comes only from direct contact after sustained inquiry
Lambda.9 is the most honest moment of aporia in the entire corpus — Aristotle approaches the limit of what can be said and does not pretend to go beyond it. The silence is not a failure but the correct response to what exceeds predication. This is the apophatic moment that the entire argument has been building toward: the investigation of being qua being arrives at what is beyond being as ordinarily understood.
Lambda.9 is the fourth position of the catuṣkoṭi instantiated at the highest philosophical level. The Unmoved Mover is neither described in ordinary predication (it cannot be) nor dismissed as unknowable (it is the most knowable). It is known by direct contact — noēsis noēseōs — not by discursive reasoning. This is the Aristotelian expression of what the Buddhist tradition calls direct knowing — the paññā that sees without conceptual elaboration. The text falls silent not because the inquiry has failed but because it has succeeded: it has arrived at the point where predication gives way to presence.

Μ–Ν — Mu-Nu: The elenchus of Platonic number theory · the fourth tetralemmic position

Μ.1–3 1076a8–1078a31
Aporia: do mathematical objects exist separately, as sensible things, or in some other way?
The three positions
Mathematical objects (numbers, lines, geometrical figures) must exist in one of three ways: (1) separately from sensible things — the Platonic position; (2) as identical with sensible things — the Pythagorean position; (3) as neither separate nor identical but somehow intermediate. All three positions face difficulties.
Resolution
Mathematical objects exist as the sensible things considered in abstraction from their sensible properties. They are not separate substances; they are not identical with the sensible things (a bronze sphere is not identical with the mathematical sphere). They exist as objects of a certain mode of intellectual attention — what Aristotle calls abstraction (ἀφαίρεσις). The mathematician studies the sensible in abstraction from its matter; the result is the mathematical object.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 510c–511a — the mathematicals as intermediate between Forms and sensibles
Pythagoreans — mathematical objects identical with sensible things; the sensible just is the numerical
Plato — Philebus 56c–57d — the two arithmetics: practical (counting sensible things) and philosophical (pure units)
Abstraction as the mode of mathematical existence is the epistemological counterpart of the per se predication structure. The mathematician considers what belongs per se to number as number — not what belongs to the bronze sphere as bronze. This is the same move as the biological classification: the biologist considers what belongs per se to the bird as bird — not what belongs to this particular bird accidentally. Abstraction is the cognitive operation that isolates per se predications from accidental ones at every level of inquiry.
Μ–Ν as a whole 1076a8–1093b29
The fourth tetralemmic position: the elenchus that clears the field for Lambda
The function of Mu-Nu
Mu-Nu constitute the fourth tetralemmic position of the Metaphysics — not a positive account of anything but the systematic elenchus of every version of the Platonic claim that abstract objects (Forms, numbers, mathematical objects) are primary substances. Mu examines the claim that mathematical objects are substances. Nu examines the claim that numbers are the principles of all things. Neither book introduces new positive content — their logical force derives entirely from their position as the dissolution of the only remaining alternative to what Lambda established.
The structure of the elenchus
Every version of Platonic mathematical ontology is examined and dissolved by the same method: the Platonic position treats what is true of mathematical objects in a secondary sense (they are objects of a certain intellectual attention, they have a kind of unity and definiteness) as if it were true in the primary sense (they are substances with separate existence). Restoring the qualification in each case: mathematical objects exist secundum quid — as objects of abstraction — not simpliciter as separately existing substances.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 525a–527c — numbers as the proper objects of the philosopher's arithmetic
Plato — Parmenides 143a–144e — the generation of number from the One
Pythagoreans — numbers as the principles of all things; the limit and the unlimited as the generative pair
Plato — Philebus 16c–18d — the one and the many as the structure of all inquiry; number as the mediating structure
Mu-Nu have no positive content of their own. Their logical force is entirely relational — they clear the field by dissolving the only remaining competitor to Lambda's account. Remove Lambda and Mu-Nu have nothing to refute. Remove Mu-Nu and Lambda is an assertion without elenctic defence. The four positions of the Metaphysics are mutually constitutive: none stands without the others. This is the tetralemmic structure at its most explicit.
Mu-Nu instantiate the fourth position of the catuṣkoṭi at the level of the Metaphysics as a whole. The fourth position — neither is nor is-not — has no positive content of its own; it is what remains when all three positive positions have been fully stated and the elenchus applied. Mu-Nu perform this function: they are the dissolution that leaves Lambda standing. The entire Metaphysics instantiates the catuṣkoṭi: Beta-Gamma (the question, position one) · Delta-Eta (the determination, position two) · Theta-Lambda (the answer, position three) · Mu-Nu (the elenctic fourth position, leaving Lambda as the only viable account of being qua being).
This document maps the secondary aporia moments — individual aporiai embedded within otherwise continuous arguments — across Gamma, Delta, Epsilon, Theta, Lambda, and Mu-Nu. Together with the three primary aporia books (Beta, Zeta, Iota) and the recapitulation (Kappa), these constitute the complete aporia apparatus of the Metaphysics. The aporia method is not confined to designated books — it runs through the entire argument as the standard mode of philosophical inquiry: pose the difficulty honestly, examine both sides without suppressing qualifications, resolve by identifying which qualification was being suppressed. The Metaphysics is one argument. The aporiai are that argument's honesty about its own difficulty. March 2026.
