Aristotle · Μετὰ τὰ Φυσικά Β — The Fifteen Aporiai

Metaphysics Beta: every aporia, its original source in Plato and the predecessors, and its resolution elsewhere in the corpus

The function of Beta: Metaphysics Beta (Book B) is not a collection of unsolved puzzles. It is the formal tetralemmic structure of the entire Metaphysics stated at the outset — the fifteen irreducible difficulties that any account of being qua being must resolve. Aristotle identifies each aporia, traces it to its source in a predecessor's position, states both sides as forcefully as possible, and leaves it unresolved — to be resolved only by the account of Books Gamma through Lambda. Beta is the elenctic opening of the argument: it establishes that every previous position is incomplete, that the question must be posed afresh, and that the answer must resolve all fifteen simultaneously. The fifteen aporiai are not fifteen separate questions. They are one question — what is being qua being? — stated from fifteen angles.
Aporia 1 993b24–994a1 · Beta Ch. 1
Does investigation of all four causes belong to one science or to several?
The difficulty
If first philosophy investigates being qua being, does it investigate all four causes — material, formal, efficient, final — or only some? If only some, which science investigates the others? If all four belong to one science, why did earlier thinkers each identify only one or two causes?
Original sources
Thales / Anaximenes / Heraclitus — material cause only (water, air, fire)
Empedocles — material (four roots) + efficient (Love and Strife) but not formal or final
Anaxagoras — material + efficient (Nous) but no formal or final analysis
Plato — Phaedo 97b–99d — Socrates' complaint that Anaxagoras did not use Nous as a final cause; the Theory of Forms introduces formal cause but inadequately grounds efficient and material
Resolution: Met. Α.3–10 (survey of causes in predecessors) · Met. Δ.1–2 (arche and aition) · Physics II.3 (the four causes as the complete account). All four causes belong to first philosophy because being qua being requires all four for a complete account.
This aporia grounds the claim that the four aitiai are not derivative of being but the routes by which we arrive at it. The predecessors' incompleteness is the incompleteness of their causal analysis.
Aporia 2 994a1–b31 · Beta Ch. 2
Do the principles of demonstration belong to first philosophy, or only the principles of substance?
The difficulty
LNC and PEM are the most universal principles — they hold for all beings. But first philosophy investigates being qua being. Does it therefore investigate the principles of demonstration (LNC, PEM) as well as the principles of substance? Or does it investigate only substance, leaving the logical principles to logic?
Original sources
Plato — Republic 510b–511d — mathematicians assume their hypotheses without examining them; the dialectician is the one who investigates the unhypothetical first principle
Plato — Parmenides 135b–136e — the exercise in dialectic as the training for examining first principles
Parmenides — being and non-being as the first principles of all reasoning; the principle that being is and non-being is not
Resolution: Met. Γ.3–8 — first philosophy investigates LNC and PEM because they are properties of being qua being, not merely of logic. The defence of LNC in Gamma is the resolution of this aporia.
This aporia is the source of the four elenchus conditions. LNC is not merely a logical principle but an ontological one — it holds because being itself is structured by the same conditions that govern genuine contradiction.
Aporia 3 995a27–b4 · Beta Ch. 1
Is there one science of all substances, or several sciences for different kinds of substance?
The difficulty
Mathematics investigates mathematical objects (numbers, geometrical figures). Physics investigates natural substances. Theology investigates the divine. Are these three sciences, or one? If one, what unifies them? If three, how can there be a single first philosophy?
Original sources
Plato — Republic 525a–531e — the mathematical sciences (arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, harmonics) as propaedeutic to dialectic; the Form of the Good as the unhypothetical principle unifying all
Pythagoreans — number as the principle of all things; mathematics as the science of being
Plato — Philebus 16c–17a — the one and the many as the structure of all inquiry
Resolution: Met. Ε.1 — first philosophy investigates being qua being and is therefore the most universal science; the other sciences investigate being under a specific restriction (natural, mathematical). The unity of first philosophy is the unity of being qua being as the subject.
Aporia 4 995b4–18 · Beta Ch. 1
Does first philosophy investigate only sensible substances, or also non-sensible ones?
The difficulty
If there are only sensible substances, first philosophy collapses into natural science. If there are non-sensible substances (Forms, mathematical objects, the divine), first philosophy must be a different kind of science — but then what is its relation to natural science?
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 65d–66a — the philosopher turns away from sensible things to the Forms; the real objects of knowledge are non-sensible
Plato — Republic 509d–511e — the divided line: the intelligible is prior to and more real than the sensible
Plato — Timaeus 27d–28a — being (the Forms) vs becoming (the sensible world); only being is the proper object of knowledge
Resolution: Met. Ε.1 and Λ.1 — there are three kinds of substance: mathematical, natural, and theological. First philosophy investigates the third — unmoved, separate — because it is primary. The sensible substances are investigated by natural science; the mathematical by mathematics.
Aporia 5 995b18–27 · Beta Ch. 1
Does first philosophy investigate substances only, or also their essential attributes (per se accidents)?
The difficulty
If first philosophy investigates being qua being, it must investigate the attributes that belong to being as such — unity, plurality, sameness, difference, likeness, contrariety, priority, posteriority. But these are attributes, not substances. Does a science of substance also investigate its per se attributes?
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 254b–259d — the five greatest kinds (being, rest, motion, same, different) and their interrelations; the philosopher must investigate the Forms and their communion
Plato — Parmenides 127e–136e — the one and the many, the same and the different, as the primary attributes requiring dialectical investigation
Resolution: Met. Γ.2 and Δ (the philosophical lexicon) — first philosophy investigates both substance and the attributes that belong to being qua being. This is confirmed by the fact that the Metaphysics includes both Zeta (substance) and Iota (unity and its relatives).
The per se attributes of being qua being are the logical conditions of the four elenchus conditions: same (subject), same (predicate), same (respect), same (time). The investigation of being's per se attributes is the investigation of the conditions of LNC.
Aporia 6 995b27–996a2 · Beta Ch. 1
Are the principles of things the genera or the immanent constituents (matter)?
The difficulty
Are the first principles the highest genera (being, one, the good — as the Platonists hold) or the immanent material constituents (earth, water, fire, air — as the Presocratics hold)? The genus is prior in the order of definition; matter is prior in the order of generation. Which priority is primary?
Original sources
Plato — Republic 505a–509c — the Form of the Good as the highest principle, beyond being and essence
Pythagoreans — the One and the Indefinite Dyad as the highest principles generating all numbers and hence all things
Anaximander — the Apeiron (the Indefinite) as the material principle from which all things arise
Empedocles — the four elements as the material principles; Love and Strife as efficient
Resolution: Met. Ζ.13 and Ζ.17 — neither genera nor matter alone are the primary principles. The form — what a thing is — is the primary principle. Genera are not substances; matter without form is not actual. The form is the cause of being (Zeta.17).
Aporia 7 996a2–b26 · Beta Ch. 2
Are the highest genera (being, one) or the lowest species (infimae species) the principles?
The difficulty
The highest genera (being, one) are predicated of everything — they are maximally universal. The lowest species are most specific — they define the individual most precisely. Which are the true principles? If the highest genera, they are too general to explain the specific. If the lowest species, they are too many and too particular to be first principles.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 505a–511e — the Form of the Good as the highest and most universal principle
Plato — Philebus 16c–18d — method of collection and division: from the one through the many to the lowest species
Antisthenes — only individuals are real; universals are mere names. The infimae species tend toward this position
Resolution: Met. Ζ.13 — universals are not substances; the highest genera (being, one) cannot be substances because they would be predicated of everything including themselves, generating paradox. Met. Ζ.17 — the form of the infima species is the primary substance, not the individual matter.
This aporia is the source of Russell's paradox in its Aristotelian form. If the highest genera are substances predicated of everything including themselves, the self-predication generates contradiction. Aristotle's resolution — the highest genera are not substances — is the categorical level-distinction that prevents the paradox.
Aporia 8 996b26–997a25 · Beta Ch. 2
Is there anything besides the particulars? Are mathematical objects real?
The difficulty
Mathematical objects (the perfect circle, the number 2) are not sensible — no sensible circle is perfectly circular. But are they separate from sensible things? If so, there is a realm of intermediate objects between the Forms and the sensible. If not, mathematics is about sensible things — but sensible things are imperfect instances of mathematical properties.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 510c–511a — the mathematicals as intermediate between Forms and sensibles; the geometer uses visible figures but thinks of the Form
Plato — Philebus 56c–57d — the distinction between practical arithmetic (counting sensible things) and philosophical arithmetic (pure units)
Pythagoreans — numbers as the real constituents of sensible things; the sensible just is the numerical
Resolution: Met. Μ.1–3 — mathematical objects are not separate substances; they are the sensible things considered in abstraction from their sensible properties. They exist in the sensible but are studied without the sensible by abstraction. This dissolves the need for a separate realm of intermediates.
Aporia 9 997a25–998a19 · Beta Ch. 2
Are the principles one in number or one in kind?
The difficulty
If the principles are one in number — if there is one single principle of all things — then all things are one and there is no plurality or change. If the principles are one in kind — if there is one type of principle with numerically many instances — how does the type unify them? And how many instances of each type are there?
Original sources
Parmenides — being is one; plurality and change are illusory. The principle (being) is one in number
Plato — Sophist 244b–245e — the examination of Parmenides' monism; the attempt to show that being cannot be simply one
Empedocles — four principles one in kind (the roots) with numerically many instances of each
Plato — Parmenides 128a–130a — the problem of participation: if the Form is one in number, how do the many participate in it?
Resolution: Met. Λ.4–5 — the principles are one in kind, not in number. Each individual has its own form and matter; the formal principles are the same in kind across individuals of the same species. 'Man generates man' — the form is one in kind; the individual instances are numerically many.
Aporia 10 998a20–999a23 · Beta Ch. 3
Do perishable and imperishable things have the same or different principles?
The difficulty
If perishable and imperishable things have the same principles, how do some things perish and others not? If they have different principles, are the principles of perishable things themselves perishable? And if so, what explains their own existence?
Original sources
Empedocles — the same four roots govern both perishable composites and the eternal cycle of combination and separation; Love and Strife are eternal but their products perish
Plato — Phaedo 78c–80b — the Forms are imperishable; the sensible things that participate in them are perishable; same principles (Forms) govern both
Plato — Timaeus 41a–d — the Demiurge creates both the eternal gods and the perishable mortals; different modes of creation for each
Resolution: Met. Λ.1–6 — there are three kinds of substance: mathematical (neither generated nor perishable in the relevant sense), natural (generated and perishable), and divine (unmoved, eternal). The principles are the same in kind but the divine substance is its own principle — pure actuality — while natural substances require both form and matter.
Aporia 11 999a24–b24 · Beta Ch. 4
Are One and Being substances, or merely attributes of other things?
The difficulty
If One and Being are substances — the most fundamental things — then everything that is, is one being. But then there is no plurality, no difference, no change. If One and Being are merely attributes of other substances, what are those substances, and how do they exist prior to being one and being?
Original sources
Parmenides — being is one and being is all there is; the identification of being with unity generates monism
Pythagoreans — the One is the primary substance from which all number and hence all being derives
Plato — Parmenides 137c–142a — the first hypothesis: if the One is, what follows? The aporia of the One as substance
Plato — Sophist 244b–245e — being cannot be simply one without generating contradiction; the examination of Eleatic monism
Resolution: Met. Γ.2 and Ι.2 — one and being are not substances but per se attributes of substance — they are said in as many ways as being is said. Every substance is one, and every one is a being, but 'one' and 'being' do not add a further substance to the world; they are transcendental properties of what already is.
This aporia directly generates Russell's paradox when One and Being are treated as substances predicated of themselves. The resolution — they are transcendental attributes, not substances — is the categorical level-distinction of Cat. Ch. 2.
Aporia 12 999b24–1000a4 · Beta Ch. 4
Do the principles exist as universals or as particulars?
The difficulty
If the principles are universals, they are predicated of many — but then they are not primary substances (Cat. Ch. 5: primary substance is individual). If the principles are particulars, they cannot be known — knowledge is always of the universal. Either the principles are unknowable, or they are not primary substances.
Original sources
Plato — Meno 85b–86b — knowledge is of the universal; the particular is only an instance
Plato — Republic 476a–480a — the lovers of sights and sounds know only particulars; the philosopher knows the Forms (universals)
Antisthenes — only the particular individual is real; the universal is merely a name (this tendency toward nominalism makes universals unknowable as principles)
Plato — Parmenides 132a–b — the Third Man argument: if the Form is a universal, and it resembles the particulars, there must be a further Form — and so on infinitely
Resolution: Met. Ζ.13 and Λ.5 — the principles are universal in kind but the form of each individual is that individual's own. Knowledge is of the universal; but what exists as a principle is the form in the individual — neither a separate Platonic universal nor a bare particular. 'Man generates man' — the universal is real but not separately existent.
This aporia is the epistemological expression of your central thesis. The truth conditions for predications are universal (the genus-species apparatus); but they apply to individuals. The universal is real — it grounds the truth conditions — but it is not a separate substance. This is exactly Post. An. I.4: per se predication of universals about individuals.
Aporia 13 1000a5–1001a3 · Beta Ch. 4
Are the principles potential or actual?
The difficulty
If the principles are only potential, then before they are actualised there is nothing — absolute non-being precedes all being. If the principles are only actual, then there is no change or generation — everything is always already what it fully is. But we observe both that things come to be and that things are fully actual. How can the principles be both?
Original sources
Megarians (Diodorus Cronus) — only what is actual is possible; potentiality is a fiction. This makes change unintelligible
Parmenides — being is fully actual and complete; there is no potentiality, no non-being, no change
Plato — Sophist 247d–248e — the battle of the Gods and Giants: materialists (only what is tangible is real = only actuality) vs the Friends of the Forms (only Forms = only a kind of actuality without matter)
Plato — Timaeus 27d–28c — being (always actual) vs becoming (always in process) — the tension between pure actuality and pure potentiality
Resolution: Met. Θ.1–9 — the actuality/potentiality distinction is the key to resolving this aporia. Principles are both: formal principles are primarily actual (form is actuality); material principles are primarily potential (matter is potentiality). Actuality is prior to potentiality (Theta.8). Motion is the actuality of what is potentially (Physics III.1) — the intermediate between pure actuality and pure potentiality.
This aporia is the ontological ground of the truth glut as dependent origination. The superposition state — neither fully actual nor fully potential — is real, not a logical error. It is motion, becoming, the ongoing event of form actualising matter. The truth glut is what incomplete actuality looks like from the logical side.
Aporia 14 1001a4–b25 · Beta Ch. 4
Are numbers, lines, figures, and points substances?
The difficulty
The Pythagoreans and Platonists treat mathematical objects as substances — the most real things. But if numbers and geometrical objects are substances, they must be either identical with sensible things or separate from them. If identical, mathematics is just natural science. If separate, there are two worlds — the sensible and the mathematical — and no account of their relation.
Original sources
Pythagoreans — number is substance; sensible things just are numerical structures; the limit and the unlimited generate all things
Plato — Republic 525a–527c — arithmeticians assume the existence of odd and even numbers as real; the philosopher investigates what number really is
Plato — Philebus 56c–57a — philosophical arithmetic (pure units, not sensible ones) vs practical arithmetic
Plato — Parmenides 143a–144e — the generation of number from the One and the Others; numbers as the primary beings
Resolution: Met. Μ.2–3 and Ν.1–6 — mathematical objects are not substances. They are the sensible things considered in abstraction from their sensible properties. Numbers do not exist separately; they are attributes of groups of things. The full elenchus of the Platonic mathematical ontology occupies Mu-Nu — the fourth tetralemmic position of the Metaphysics.
Aporia 15 1001b26–1002b11 · Beta Ch. 5
Do the Forms exist as Plato claims?
The difficulty
If the Forms exist separately from sensible things, how do they cause anything? They cannot be efficient causes (they do not move anything), material causes (sensible things are not made of Forms), or final causes in any clear sense. They are supposed to be formal causes — but then how does participation work? And the Third Man argument shows that postulating a separate Form generates an infinite regress.
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 74a–77a — the Equal Itself, the Beautiful Itself, the Good Itself — separately existing Forms as the causes of the properties of sensible things
Plato — Republic 476a–480a, 507b–511e — the Forms as the real objects of knowledge; the Form of the Good as the highest
Plato — Parmenides 130e–135b — Parmenides' own critique of the Theory of Forms: the Third Man argument, the problem of participation, the problem of knowledge of Forms
Plato — Timaeus 51b–52d — the Forms as the paradigms used by the Demiurge in creating sensible things
Plato — Symposium 210e–211b — the Beautiful Itself, eternal, unchanging, separate — the culmination of the ascent
Resolution: Met. Ζ.6, Ζ.13–16, Μ.4–5, Ν.2–6 — the Forms as separately existing universals are not substances. Form does exist — but as the immanent form of individual substances, not as a separately existing Platonic entity. The elenchus of the Theory of Forms occupies multiple books of the Metaphysics; its definitive completion is in Mu-Nu. Lambda.7 gives the only acceptable account of a separately existing form: the Unmoved Mover — pure actuality, self-thinking thought, which is not predicated of anything else and does not participate in anything else.
This is the culminating aporia. Its resolution is the entire Metaphysics. The Form exists — but as immanent form (Zeta) and as pure actuality at the limit (Lambda). The Platonic Form is both right (form is real and causally primary) and wrong (form is not separately existent). The correction preserves what is true in Plato while dissolving the paradoxes that separate existence generates. Mu-Nu perform the elenchus; Lambda gives the resolution. Together they constitute the fourth tetralemmic position of the Metaphysics.
This document maps all fifteen aporiai of Metaphysics Beta to their original sources in Plato and the predecessors, and gives their resolution elsewhere in the Aristotelian corpus. The aporiai are not independent puzzles — they are one question (what is being qua being?) stated from fifteen angles. The resolution of all fifteen simultaneously is the argument of Gamma through Lambda. Mu-Nu perform the elenchus of the last remaining competitor (Platonism) and leave Lambda standing. The four tetralemmic positions of the Metaphysics: Α–Γ (the question) · Δ–Η (the determination) · Θ–Λ (the answer) · Μ–Ν (the elenchus). March 2026.
