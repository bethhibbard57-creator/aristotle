Aristotle · Μετὰ τὰ Φυσικά Ζ — The Sustained Aporia of Being

Metaphysics Zeta: all seventeen chapters · each candidate for being · why each fails or partially survives · the original sources · the resolution

The function of Zeta: Metaphysics Zeta is the most difficult sustained argument in the Western philosophical tradition. It takes the question left standing by Beta — what is primary being? — and works through every candidate with ruthless precision. The candidates are: the substratum (matter) · the universal · the genus · the essence (τὸ τί ἦν εἶναι, the what-it-was-to-be). Each is examined, each is found insufficient in some respect, until in Zeta.17 the form — understood as the cause of being, not as a separately existing Platonic entity — is left standing as the only viable answer. Zeta does not proceed by simple elimination. Each candidate is partially right; the error in each case is a suppression of a qualification — treating what is true of the candidate in a secondary sense as if it were true in the primary sense. The chapter by chapter movement of Zeta is the most sustained application in the corpus of the method of the Sophistical Refutations: detecting and dissolving the suppressed qualification at every level of the analysis of being.
Ζ.1 1028a10–b7
Being is said in many ways — but being is the primary sense
orienting
The move
Being is said in many ways (the categories) but all the other senses — quality, quantity, relation, place, time, and the rest — are said to be only by reference to being. Being is the primary sense of being; the others are secondary. Therefore the investigation of being qua being is primarily the investigation of being.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 255c–d — being and the other greatest kinds; being is not reducible to any single kind
Parmenides — being is one and primary; the others are derivative. Aristotle agrees that being is primary but disagrees that it is one
Plato — Republic 509b — the Form of the Good is beyond being and essence — being has a primary sense toward which all else is oriented
This chapter establishes the pros hen (toward one) relation that resolves Beta Aporia 3: being is said in many ways but all ways are related to one primary sense — being. First philosophy is therefore primarily the investigation of being.
The pros hen relation is the ontological ground of the per se / accidental distinction. All categories are said to be by reference to being — which means the truth conditions for all predications are ultimately grounded in the categorical placement of the subject as a being.
Ζ.2 1028b8–1029a34
What are the candidates for being?
survey
The candidates listed
Bodies (sensible — animals, plants, the elements) · the parts of bodies · the heavenly bodies · mathematical objects (numbers, lines, surfaces) · the Forms. Most people recognise only sensible beings; the Platonists add Forms and mathematicals. Aristotle will examine all candidates.
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 78c–80b — the Forms as the real beings; sensible things merely participate
Pythagoreans — numbers as the real beings underlying all things
Plato — Republic 509d–511e — the hierarchy: Forms (most real) · mathematicals (intermediate) · sensible things (least real)
The survey establishes the field. Zeta will work through each candidate. The sensible beings are examined in Zeta.3–16; the Forms and mathematicals are examined in Zeta.13–16 and fully in Mu-Nu.
Ζ.3 1028b34–1029b12
Candidate 1: The substratum (ὑποκείμενον) — matter as being
matter alone — fails
The candidate
Being is the substratum — what underlies everything else. If we strip away all qualities, all quantities, all relations, what remains is the bare substratum — prime matter. This is being in the most fundamental sense: what everything else is predicated of, and which is not itself predicated of anything.
Why it fails
Prime matter — the bare substratum stripped of all qualities — is neither a this (τόδε τι, a determinate individual) nor a quantity nor a quality nor anything else. It has no being of its own; it is pure potentiality. Something that can exist in no way independently cannot be being in the primary sense. Being must be capable of separate existence; pure matter cannot exist separately.
Original sources
Anaximander — the Apeiron (Indefinite) as the underlying substratum of all things; unlimited, formless matter as the primary being
Plato — Timaeus 49a–52d — the Receptacle (χώρα) as the formless nurse of becoming; the closest Platonic analogue to prime matter as substratum
Anaximenes — air as the underlying substratum; all things are transformations of one material principle
Matter fails as primary being because it cannot exist separately and determinately. But matter is not eliminated — it is the material cause, a genuine constituent of being. The error is treating what is true of matter in a secondary sense (it underlies everything) as if it were the primary sense of being.
This is the secundum quid move applied to the analysis of being. Matter is a genuine constituent of being — but treating it as the primary being suppresses the qualification 'as the material cause'. The suppression generates the position of the materialists.
Ζ.4–5 1029b1–1031a14
Candidate 2: The essence (τὸ τί ἦν εἶναι) — what it was to be something
essence — survives, with qualification
The candidate
The essence — τὸ τί ἦν εἶναι, literally 'the what it was to be for something' — is what is expressed in the definition. It is what a thing is in virtue of itself, not in virtue of something else. Every thing that has a definition has an essence; the essence is what the definition expresses.
The qualification required
Essence belongs primarily to being — it is most properly the essence of a being. Other things (qualities, quantities) have essences only in a secondary sense — their essences are always defined by reference to a being. 'White' has an essence only as 'the essence of white surface'. The essence is not the same as the thing in every case — only in the case of primary beings (Zeta.6).
Original sources
Plato — Meno 72b–73c — the search for what virtue is — its essence — as the proper object of definition; the essence is what is common to all instances
Plato — Phaedo 65d–66a — the Equal Itself, the Beautiful Itself — the essence apprehended by thought alone, without the senses
Plato — Republic 507b — the Form is the essence of each thing; the essence is what the Form is
The essence survives as a strong candidate — especially for primary beings, where the thing and its essence are identical (Zeta.6). The analysis of essence in Zeta.4–5 establishes the logical apparatus for the rest of Zeta: what has an essence has a definition; what has a definition is knowable per se; what is knowable per se is being in the primary sense.
The essence is the formal expression of the per se. To know the essence of a thing is to know which predicates are per se true of it — necessarily, always, in virtue of what it is. This is Post. An. I.4 applied to the metaphysical level: essence and per se predication are the same thing from two angles — the ontological (essence) and the logical (per se predication).
Ζ.6 1031a15–1032a10
Is each thing identical with its essence?
yes — for primary beings
The argument
For primary beings — individual men, individual horses — the thing and its essence are identical. Socrates just is what it is to be Socrates. There is no gap between the individual being and its essence — no further thing that makes the individual what it is. For accidental compounds (the pale man) the thing and its essence are not identical — 'the pale man' and 'what it is to be a pale man' are different.
Original sources
Plato — Parmenides 130b–131e — the problem of self-predication: does the Form of Large participate in itself? If each Form is identical with its essence, does the Form of Man belong to the Form of Man? The Third Man regress begins here
Plato — Phaedo 100c–d — things are beautiful by the Beautiful Itself — the Form and the essence are identified, generating self-predication
The identity of a primary being with its essence resolves the Third Man regress: there is no gap between Socrates and what it is to be Socrates into which a further Form could be inserted. The regress only arises when the essence is treated as a separately existing Platonic Form rather than as immanent in the individual.
The identity of primary being with its essence is the ontological ground of the categorical placement as truth-condition maker. To know that Socrates is a man is to know his essence — and from his essence all per se predicates follow. The essence is not a separate entity but the formal structure of the individual itself.
Ζ.7–9 1032a11–1034b19
How do beings come to be? The role of form, matter, and the efficient cause in generation
form as the cause of coming-to-be
The argument
When a being comes to be — when a man is generated, when a house is built — what is generated is not the form (form does not come to be) and not the matter (matter persists through generation). What comes to be is the compound of form and matter. The form is the principle of the compound; it is what the efficient cause (the builder, the father) impresses on the matter.
The key claim
The form is not itself generated. There is no generation of the form; the form is always already present in the efficient cause — 'man generates man'. The builder has the form of the house in his soul before building; the father has the form of man in his body before generating. The form is transmitted, not created.
Original sources
Plato — Timaeus 28a–29d — the Demiurge fashions the world by imposing form (the Forms as paradigms) on matter (the Receptacle); the closest Platonic account of generation through form imposition
Plato — Phaedo 96a–100c — Socrates' account of generation through Forms; things come to be by participation in the relevant Form
Zeta.7–9 establishes the biological parallel: GA I and II confirm that the form is transmitted through the semen (the efficient cause carrying the formal principle) to the female matter. 'Man generates man' is the biological confirmation of the metaphysical claim that form is not itself generated.
The form is not generated — this is the ontological ground of the claim that species are eternal and universals are real. The individual perishes; the form persists through generation. This is the Aristotelian answer to the aporia of universals: they are real (they persist) but not separately existent (they persist only through the causal transmission of individual generation).
Ζ.10–11 1034b20–1037b7
Are the parts of the definition parts of the thing defined? The problem of parts and wholes
form only — not material parts — in the definition
The aporia
The definition of man includes 'rational animal' — it does not include 'flesh and bones'. But man is made of flesh and bones. Are flesh and bones parts of the definition or not? If not, how is the definition complete? If yes, why doesn't the definition of the circle include bronze (if the circle is bronze)?
The resolution
The definition expresses the formal parts — the genus and differentia — not the material parts. Flesh and bones are the matter required for the form to be realised (hypothetical necessity) but they are not parts of the form itself. The definition of man is the definition of the form of man; the material is presupposed but not included.
Original sources
Plato — Meno 77b–79e — failed attempts at definition that include material or accidental parts; Socrates' demand for the essence without the material
Plato — Theaetetus 207a–208b — the problem of the syllable: is the syllable just its letters or something over and above them? The parts/whole problem for definitions
This establishes the formal-material distinction at the level of definition. The definition expresses the form; the matter is the hypothetically necessary medium for the form's realisation. This connects directly to Physics II.9 (hypothetical necessity) and PA I.1 (the method of biological definition).
Ζ.12 1037b8–1038a35
Why is the definition one? What unifies the genus and differentia?
the last differentia contains all previous ones
The aporia
A definition has multiple parts — genus and differentia. What makes it one definition rather than a conjunction of two separate claims? 'Two-footed animal' — why is this one thing, not two (two-footed + animal)?
The resolution
In a properly constructed definition the last differentia contains all the previous ones. 'Two-footed' already presupposes 'footed', which presupposes 'land-dwelling', which presupposes 'animal'. The last differentia is not added to the genus — it specifies it completely. The definition is one because the last differentia is the complete specification of what the genus is in this case.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 218d–221c — the method of division: how to arrive at the final definition of the Angler by successive differentiation; the problem of whether the definition is one
Plato — Statesman 258b–267c — the method of division applied to the Statesman; the same problem of definitional unity
This connects to Topics VI.6: a definition must be one, not a conjunction. The unity of the definition is the unity of the form — the form is what makes the compound one rather than a mere aggregate. This prepares for Zeta.17's claim that the form is the cause of being — the cause of the compound's unity.
Ζ.13–14 1038b1–1039b19
Candidate 3: The universal — is the universal being?
universal as separate being — fails
The candidate
The Platonists hold that the universal — the Form — is being in the highest sense. The Form of Man is more truly being than any individual man because it is eternal, unchanging, and the cause of the individual's being what it is.
Why it fails
No universal is a being. If the universal Man were a being, it would be a being predicated of many individual men — but then it would be both one (as a universal) and many (as predicated of many), generating contradiction. Moreover, the being of each thing is its own — it is not shared. But a universal is shared by many; what is shared by many cannot be the being (the what-it-is) of any one of them specifically.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 596a — the Form as the one over the many; the one Form corresponding to each class of things
Plato — Parmenides 130b–135b — Parmenides' Third Man argument: if the Form is a universal predicated of particulars, and it resembles them, there must be a further Form. Plato himself raises the regress
Plato — Phaedo 78d — the Form is one, not many; the Beauty Itself is one; yet it is shared by all beautiful things
The universal is not being — but the universal is real. This is the resolution of Beta Aporia 12. The universal exists but not as a separately predicated being. It exists as the form immanent in the individual — the formal cause of the individual, not a separate entity over and above it.
This is the central anti-Platonic move. The universal is real — it grounds per se predication and scientific knowledge. But it is not a separately existing being. The truth conditions for 'Socrates is a man' are grounded in the form immanent in Socrates — not in a separately existing Form of Man. Russell's type theory makes the same move from the logical side: levels of predication prevent self-predication. Aristotle makes it from the ontological side: the form is immanent, not separate.
Ζ.15 1039b20–1040b4
Can individuals be defined? Are the Forms definable?
individual as such — not definable · Form as separate — not definable
The aporia
Definition is of the universal and the necessary — it cannot capture the individual as such (this man, this horse at this moment). But if the Forms are individuals (each Form is one and unique), as Plato seems to hold, then the Forms cannot be defined either. Yet definition is supposed to be the expression of the Form's essence.
The resolution
Definition is of the species-form, not of the individual matter. The individual is known through perception and through the species-form — we know this man because he is a man and because we perceive him. The species-form is definable; the individual matter is not. The Platonic Forms, if they are truly individual (unique), cannot be defined — which refutes the claim that they are the primary objects of knowledge.
Original sources
Plato — Seventh Letter 342a–343a — the problem of names, definitions, images, and knowledge; the difficulty of expressing the essence in language
Plato — Parmenides 130b — are there Forms of individuals (Socrates, this man)? Plato hesitates
This prepares for Zeta.17's positive account: definition is of the form — the species-form immanent in the individual. The individual as such (this matter) is not definable; the form it instantiates is. This is the resolution of the tension between particular knowledge (perception) and universal knowledge (definition).
Ζ.16 1040b5–1041a5
Candidate 4: The genus — is the genus being?
genus as separate being — fails · genus as part of definition — survives
The argument
The parts of animals (bones, flesh, blood) and the elements (earth, water, fire, air) are not beings in the primary sense — they are not separable from the compounds they constitute. They are beings only potentially — as constituents of actual compounds. The genus is real but it is not primary being — it is the universal aspect of the form, not the form itself.
Original sources
Empedocles — the four elements as the primary beings from which all compound bodies are made; the roots are the primary beings
Plato — Timaeus 53c–56c — the four elements as the primary bodies; the mathematical structures (triangles) as their constitutive principles
The genus fails as primary being for the same reason as matter: it cannot exist separately and determinately. The genus is real — it is part of the definition — but it exists only as the universal aspect of the form in the individual, not as a separate entity.
Ζ.17 1041a6–b33
The resolution: form as the cause of being — why the compound is one
FORM — the cause of being — survives as the answer
The resolution
Being must be a cause and a principle. The right question is not 'what is man made of?' but 'why is this matter man?' — why are these bones and flesh a man rather than a heap? The answer is the form — the formal cause that makes the matter be what it is. The form is the cause of being: it is what makes the compound one rather than many, actual rather than merely potential, this kind of thing rather than that.
The key formulation
Form (εἶδος) is the cause of being (αἴτιον τοῦ εἶναι). It is not the matter, not the compound, not the universal as separately existing — it is the principle in virtue of which the matter is actually this kind of thing. The form is the answer to the why-question: why is this matter a man? Because the form of man is present in it.
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 97b–99d — Socrates' account of the true cause: not the bones and sinews but the good — the final cause as the true explanation. Aristotle takes this insight but reformulates it: the formal cause (not the Platonic Good) is the cause of being
Plato — Meno 72b — the search for what makes all instances of virtue one — the single form that explains the unity; the same structure Aristotle identifies in Zeta.17
Zeta.17 is the hinge of the entire Metaphysics. The form as the cause of being is the answer to Beta's first aporia (which causes belong to first philosophy?) — all four causes do, because form unifies them: the form is the formal cause, the expression of the final cause, and (as transmitted through generation) the efficient cause. Lambda.7 will show that in the Unmoved Mover all three coincide completely.
Form is the cause of being — this is the ontological statement of the thesis. The truth conditions for predications about beings are grounded in the form immanent in the individual. The form is what the per se predicate tracks. The categorical classification of the biological works — which gives truth values for the actual — is the empirical expression of this ontological claim. Every per se predicate is true of a subject because the subject's form makes it so. Zeta.17 is the metaphysical foundation of the entire logical and biological argument.
The movement of Zeta — what survives and what fails:

Failed candidates: Matter alone (Zeta.3) — cannot exist separately · The universal as separately existing being (Zeta.13–14) — if universal, then shared, not one's own · The genus as primary being (Zeta.16) — same reason as the universal · Individuals as definable (Zeta.15) — the individual as such is not definable

Surviving candidates (with qualification): The essence (Zeta.4–5) — survives as the formal expression of the per se · The identity of primary being with its essence (Zeta.6) — survives for primary beings · The form as immanent in the individual (Zeta.7–9, 17) — survives as the cause of being

The resolution: Form — the immanent formal cause — is primary being. Not separately existing (Plato's error). Not bare matter (the Presocratic error). Not the universal as a separate entity. But the form in the individual, which is the cause of the individual's being what it is, which is what the definition expresses, and which grounds all per se predication.
Metaphysics Zeta is the most sustained philosophical argument in the Aristotelian corpus — seventeen chapters working through every candidate for being with systematic precision. Each candidate fails by the same move: the secundum quid fallacy applied to being. What is true of each candidate in a secondary sense (matter underlies everything; the universal is knowable; the genus is common to many) is treated as if it were true in the primary sense. Restoring the qualification in each case — this is being in a secondary sense, not the primary sense — leaves the form as the only viable candidate. Zeta.17 states the answer: form is the cause of being. Everything in the Metaphysics before Zeta prepares for this; everything after Zeta (Eta, Theta, Iota, Lambda) deepens and extends it. March 2026.
