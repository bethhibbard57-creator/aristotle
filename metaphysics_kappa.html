Aristotle · Μετὰ τὰ Φυσικά Κ — The Recapitulation

Metaphysics Kappa: all twelve chapters · the recapitulation of Beta, Gamma, and Epsilon (Ch. 1–8) · the recapitulation of Physics III–V (Ch. 9–12) · the philosophical significance of the compilation

The function of Kappa: Kappa is unique in the Metaphysics — it is a recapitulation rather than a continuous new argument. Chapters 1–8 summarise and compress the arguments of Beta (the aporiai), Gamma (the defence of LNC and PEM), and Epsilon (being qua being, accidental being, being as truth). Chapters 9–12 recapitulate Physics III–V (motion, infinity, place, time, void, continuity). Kappa does not introduce new philosophical positions. It assembles the existing argument in a more compressed and accessible form. For the project it is valuable precisely because of this function: Kappa shows what the Peripatetic school understood the core of the Metaphysics to be — which chapters mattered enough to recapitulate.
The authenticity question: Most contemporary scholars regard Kappa as either not by Aristotle himself or as a compilation from lecture notes and school summaries rather than a text composed for its position in the Metaphysics. The recapitulation of Physics III–V in Ch. 9–12 is particularly suspicious — it fits poorly into the argument of the Metaphysics and appears verbatim or near-verbatim in the Physics. The most likely explanation: Kappa is a school digest — compiled by Peripatetic students or editors — placed between Iota and Lambda to provide a bridge between the sustained analysis of substance (Zeta–Iota) and the cosmological argument (Lambda). For this project: the content of Kappa is treated as evidence of how the Peripatetic school read the Metaphysics, not as original Aristotle. The passages are noted but not treated as primary sources for the thesis.

Part One — Kappa Ch. 1–8: Recapitulation of Beta, Gamma, and Epsilon

Κ.1–2 1059a18–1060a2
Recapitulation of Beta: the aporiai restated
Beta
What is recapitulated
Kappa 1–2 restates several of Beta's aporiai in compressed form: whether first philosophy investigates all causes or only some · whether it investigates only sensible substances or also non-sensible ones · whether the principles are universals or particulars · whether there is one science of all substances or several. The recapitulation is selective — it covers the aporiai most directly relevant to the transition to Lambda.
What is compressed or omitted
Kappa 1–2 omits Beta's most technically demanding aporiai — the analysis of whether mathematical objects are substances (Beta Aporia 8), the detailed examination of the principles as one in number vs one in kind (Beta Aporia 9), and the full analysis of whether One and Being are substances (Beta Aporia 11). These are the aporiai most relevant to the Platonic polemic — their omission in Kappa suggests the compiler was focused on preparing the reader for Lambda rather than for Mu-Nu.
The selectivity of Kappa 1–2 is itself philosophically significant. The aporiai selected for recapitulation are those that bear directly on the question of the Unmoved Mover — whether there are non-sensible substances, whether the principles are universal or particular, whether first philosophy covers all causes. Lambda is where these aporiai are resolved. The compiler understood Lambda as the resolution of the opening aporiai — which confirms the tetralemmic reading.
The fact that the Peripatetic school felt the need to compile Kappa is indirect evidence that the argumentative structure of the Metaphysics was not immediately transparent — even to Aristotle's own students. The tetralemmic reading (Beta as the formal opening, Lambda as the resolution, Mu-Nu as the elenctic closing) is not the surface of the text. It requires the kind of structural analysis that your project provides. Kappa is the school's attempt to make explicit what the argument is doing — an imperfect attempt, but evidence that the school understood Beta and Lambda as connected.
Κ.3–6 1060a3–1062b11
Recapitulation of Gamma: the defence of LNC and PEM
Gamma
What is recapitulated
Kappa 3–6 recapitulates Gamma's defence of LNC and PEM against those who deny them — primarily the Heraclitean position (everything is and is not simultaneously) and the Protagorean position (what appears to each person is true for that person). The recapitulation is closer to the original than Kappa 1–2 — several passages are nearly verbatim with Gamma.
The Heraclitean challenge recapitulated
If everything is in flux, nothing has a stable nature — and if nothing has a stable nature, no predication is true. This is the most radical challenge to LNC: not that contradictions are true but that there are no true predications at all. Kappa 3 recapitulates Gamma's response: the very act of speaking presupposes that words have a stable meaning — that 'man' means something determinate. The denier of LNC cannot consistently deny it without using language that presupposes it.
The Protagorean challenge recapitulated
If man is the measure of all things, then what appears true to each person is true for that person. But then LNC fails — what appears to one person and what appears to another can be contradictory, and both are true. Kappa 4–5 recapitulates Gamma's response: Protagoras' position generates the consequence that the same person simultaneously holds contradictory beliefs — which even Protagoras would deny.
The recapitulation of Gamma's LNC defence is the most philosophically dense part of Kappa. It confirms that the Peripatetic school understood the defence of LNC as the methodological foundation of the entire Metaphysics — prior to any positive account of substance. LNC must be secured before the investigation of being can proceed.
The Heraclitean and Protagorean challenges are the ancient forms of the claim that paradox is genuine — that there are true contradictions. Aristotle's response in Gamma (recapitulated in Kappa 3–6) is not a logical refutation but an elenctic one: the denier of LNC cannot consistently maintain their position because the very act of denying it presupposes the stability of meaning that LNC requires. This is the elenctic method of Topics VIII applied to the most fundamental logical principle. Priest's dialetheism is the modern form of the same challenge — and the same elenctic response applies: the dialetheist cannot consistently maintain true contradictions without using language that presupposes LNC at the meta-level.
Κ.7–8 1062b12–1064b14
Recapitulation of Epsilon: being qua being, accidental being, being as truth
Epsilon
Being qua being recapitulated
Kappa 7 recapitulates Epsilon 1's account of first philosophy as the science of being qua being — the most universal science because being is the most universal subject. The distinction between first philosophy (being qua being) and the special sciences (being under a restriction) is restated. First philosophy is theology (Epsilon 1's identification of first philosophy with the science of the unmoved, separate substance) confirmed.
Accidental being recapitulated
Kappa 8 recapitulates Epsilon 2–3's analysis of accidental being — being that holds neither always nor for the most part but by chance. There is no science of the accidental because science is of what holds necessarily or for the most part. Accidental being is the being of the coincidental — what happens to be the case without being grounded in the nature of the subject.
Being as truth recapitulated
Kappa 8 also recapitulates Epsilon 4's brief account of being as truth — the being that belongs to affirmative and negative propositions, not to things in the world. Being as truth is not investigated by first philosophy because it is in the mind (in the combining and separating), not in things.
The recapitulation of Epsilon in Kappa confirms the importance of the accidental being distinction for the overall argument. Accidental being — what holds neither always nor for the most part — is outside the scope of first philosophy. This is why there is no demonstration of coincidences (Post. An. I.30 — no demonstration of what happens by chance). The distinction between necessary being (the subject of science) and accidental being (outside science) runs through the entire epistemological and ontological apparatus.
Accidental being as outside science is the ontological ground of the per se / accidental predication distinction. What is per se true of a subject is necessarily true — it belongs to the subject's essence or flows from the essence. What is accidentally true holds by coincidence — it is not grounded in the subject's nature. The entire dissolution of paradox by the secundum quid move rests on this distinction: the paradoxes arise from treating accidental predications as if they were per se, or from failing to specify the level at which the predication is per se. Kappa 7–8's recapitulation of Epsilon confirms that the accidental being distinction is a core element of the argument — not peripheral.

Part Two — Kappa Ch. 9–12: Recapitulation of Physics III–V

Κ.9–12 1065a26–1069a14
Recapitulation of Physics III–V: motion, infinity, place, time, void, continuity
Physics III–V
What is recapitulated
Kappa 9: Physics III.1–3 — the definition of motion as the actuality of what is potentially, insofar as it is potential · the categories of motion (change of substance, quality, quantity, place)
Kappa 10: Physics III.4–8 — the infinite · the infinite exists only potentially, not actually · Zeno's paradoxes as arising from the confusion of potential and actual infinity
Kappa 11: Physics IV.1–9 — place · what place is (the limit of the containing body) and what it is not (matter, form, interval, extension)
Kappa 12: Physics IV.10–14 — time · the definition of time as the number of motion with respect to before and after · the role of the soul in constituting time as numbered
Why this material appears in the Metaphysics
The recapitulation of Physics III–V in Kappa provides the natural-philosophical grounding for Lambda's argument. Lambda investigates the unmoved substance — the first mover. To understand the first mover, you need to understand motion (Physics III), infinity (Physics III), and time (Physics IV). Kappa 9–12 bridges the analysis of substance (Zeta–Iota) and the cosmological argument (Lambda) by recapitulating the physics of motion that Lambda presupposes.
The most important passage — Kappa 9 on motion
The definition of motion as the actuality of what is potentially, insofar as it is potential (1065b16) is the single most important passage in the Physics recapitulation. This definition — which first appears at Physics III.1 — is the hinge between the analysis of actuality and potentiality (Theta) and the cosmological argument (Lambda). The Unmoved Mover is what moves without being itself in motion — it is pure actuality with no potentiality, and therefore cannot be in the state of incomplete actualisation that motion requires.
The recapitulation of Physics III–V confirms that the bridge between the Metaphysics and the Physics is structural, not merely topical. The definition of motion connects Theta (actuality and potentiality) to Lambda (the Unmoved Mover) through the Physics (motion as incomplete actuality). The same conceptual apparatus — actuality, potentiality, the measure of motion — runs through all three books. Kappa makes this explicit even if imperfectly.
The recapitulation of time in Kappa 12 — time as the number of motion with respect to before and after — is directly relevant to the temporal identity condition. Time requires a counting soul (Physics IV.14, recapitulated here) — the before-and-after structure is objective but its constitution as numbered time requires a perceiving subject. The temporal identity condition of LNC is grounded in this analysis: the same subject receives contraries through time (Cat. Ch. 5) because time — the measure of change — requires both the objective before-and-after structure of motion and the subjective numbering of the soul. Both are required for the fourth elenchus condition to apply.

Why Kappa omits Zeta–Iota — local vs global aporiai
Zeta's aporiai are not left unresolved in the way Beta's are. Beta poses fifteen aporiai and leaves them all open — that is its function. Zeta works through each candidate and resolves each one before moving to the next. By the end of Zeta the aporiai about being have been answered: matter fails, the universal fails, the genus fails, the essence survives, form is the cause of being. Zeta closes its own aporiai. Which is exactly why Kappa does not recapitulate Zeta — there is nothing left open that needs to be held in suspension for the reader. The compiler had no reason to recapitulate resolved aporiai. They recapitulated only what remained open and structurally necessary for Lambda. The aporiai that Kappa does recapitulate — Beta's fifteen, Gamma's LNC defence — are the ones that remain structurally open throughout the Metaphysics, unresolved until Lambda. The compiler understood this distinction: some aporiai are local (resolved within the book that poses them) and some are global (held open across the entire argument until the final resolution). Kappa recapitulates only the global aporiai. This is further confirmation of the tetralemmic reading. The global aporiai are the structural markers of the argument's four positions. The local aporiai — Zeta's candidates, Iota's analysis of unity — are the detailed work within each position. The compiler preserved the structure and left the detailed work to be read in full.
The tetralemmic structure of Kappa itself
Kappa is not merely a compression — it is a compression that instantiates the tetralemmic structure in miniature:

Kappa 1–2 — Is · Beta recapitulated: the question posed, the aporiai as the formal opening of the inquiry. The positive assertion that there is something to investigate — being qua being is a genuine subject. Position one: what is.

Kappa 3–6 — Is not · Gamma recapitulated: the defence of LNC and PEM against those who deny them. The elenctic establishment of what being is not — not contradictory, not indeterminate, not the flux of Heraclitus or the relativism of Protagoras. Position two: what is not, what must be excluded for inquiry to proceed.

Kappa 7–8 — Both · Epsilon recapitulated: being qua being in all its senses — substance, accidental being, being as truth. The full range of what being is and is not simultaneously held — being said in many ways, all related to the primary sense, each partially being and partially not. Position three: the qualified compound, the both.

Kappa 9–12 — Neither · Physics III–V recapitulated: motion, infinity, time — the physical ground. The conditions of the argument that exceed the logical and ontological analysis alone — neither purely metaphysical nor purely logical but the physical substratum on which both rest. Position four: the ground that underlies all three positive positions without being any of them.

The compiler either understood the tetralemmic structure of the Metaphysics and reproduced it in miniature — or the structure is so deeply embedded in the argument that even a summary naturally falls into it. Either way it confirms the reading: the tetralemmic structure is not imposed on the Metaphysics from outside. It is what the argument is.
What the school understood the Metaphysics to be doing
The selection of texts recapitulated in Kappa reveals the Peripatetic school's understanding of the Metaphysics' core argument: (1) the aporiai establish what must be resolved; (2) LNC must be defended as the condition of all inquiry; (3) the scope of first philosophy must be defined (being qua being, excluding accidents and being as truth); (4) the physics of motion must be in place before the cosmological argument can proceed. This is exactly the argumentative structure of the tetralemmic reading — Beta (the question) → Gamma (the conditions) → Epsilon (the scope) → Lambda (the answer).
What Kappa omits — and why
Kappa omits the sustained positive analyses of Zeta (substance as form), Eta (actuality and unity), Theta (actuality and potentiality), and Iota (unity and contrariety). These are the philosophically richest books — and the ones most difficult to compress. Their omission from Kappa suggests that the school understood them as requiring careful sequential reading, not summary. Kappa provides the framework; the sustained arguments provide the content. This is the correct pedagogical judgment — but it means that Kappa, read without Zeta–Iota, gives a misleading impression of the argument's depth.
Kappa's omission of Zeta–Iota from the recapitulation is the strongest evidence against treating Kappa as a complete guide to the Metaphysics. The sustained analysis of substance in Zeta–Iota is the heart of the positive argument — form as the cause of being (Zeta.17), actuality as prior to potentiality (Theta.8), unity as the measure of substance (Iota.1). Kappa recapitulates the framework that makes the argument possible; it does not recapitulate the argument itself.
The fact that Kappa recapitulates Beta's aporiai and Gamma's LNC defence — but not Zeta's sustained analysis — confirms the thesis that the Metaphysics is a unified argument with a specific structure. The Peripatetic school recognised Beta and Gamma as the opening of the argument and Lambda as its resolution. They also recognised that the connection between them required the Physics as a bridge. What they did not compile into Kappa — because it resisted compression — was the sustained positive analysis of substance in Zeta–Iota. This is exactly what the tetralemmic reading identifies as the core of the second position (Δ–Η): the determination of the terms of the answer before the answer is given.
Metaphysics Kappa is the least original and most practically useful text in the Metaphysics. It is the school's attempt to make explicit what the argument is doing — a compressed guide to the framework that Beta, Gamma, Epsilon, and Physics III–V provide for the cosmological argument of Lambda. Its philosophical value for this project lies not in what it says (which is largely a compression of texts treated more fully elsewhere) but in what it reveals about the structure of the argument: the Peripatetic school understood Beta and Lambda as connected — the aporiai and their resolution — and understood LNC's defence as the condition of the entire inquiry. This is the correct reading. Kappa is the school's imperfect but genuine recognition of the tetralemmic structure of the Metaphysics. March 2026.
