Aristotle · Μετὰ τὰ Φυσικά Ι — The Sustained Aporia of Unity

Metaphysics Iota: all ten chapters · the aporia of unity and its relatives · sameness, likeness, equality, contrariety, difference · the resolution

The function of Iota: Metaphysics Iota takes up the investigation of unity — one of the two transcendental attributes of being (the other is being itself, investigated in Gamma). If every being is one, and one is convertible with being, what is unity? Is it a substance, an attribute, or a measure? And what are the relatives of unity — sameness, likeness, equality — and the relatives of its contrary, plurality — difference, unlikeness, inequality, contrariety? Iota is the logical complement to Zeta: where Zeta investigated what primary substance is, Iota investigates what makes a substance one rather than many. The answer — the form is what unifies the compound — completes the argument of Zeta.17. Iota also contains the most precise analysis in the corpus of the structure of contrariety — which is directly relevant to the analysis of LNC, the elenchus conditions, and the dissolution of paradox.
Ι.1 1052a15–1053a30
What is unity? The four senses of one — and the measure as the primary sense
unity as measure — the primary sense identified
The four senses of one
One as continuous — what is continuous (a line, a body, a time) is one in the sense that its parts cannot be separated without destroying it
One as whole — what has a single form is one as a whole (a shoe, a man)
One as individual — what is numerically one, indivisible as a this
One as universal — what is one in definition, one in species (all men are one in species)
The primary sense
The primary sense of one is the measure — the unit by which a quantity is measured. One is what is indivisible in the primary sense of the relevant kind: one man, one step, one note. The measure varies by domain — the unit of number is different from the unit of length — but the measuring function is constant.
Original sources
Pythagoreans — the One as the first principle; all numbers arise from the One and the Indefinite Dyad; the One is the measure of all number
Plato — Parmenides 137c–142a — the first hypothesis: if the One is, what can be said of it? The most sustained analysis of unity in the Platonic corpus
Plato — Philebus 14c–18d — the one and the many as the pervasive structure of all inquiry; the method of collection and division as the management of this structure
The measure account of unity resolves Beta Aporia 11: one is not a substance but a transcendental attribute — what is said of every being insofar as it is a being. Every substance is one (has a measure) but one does not add a further substance. This is why the highest genera (being, one) are not themselves substances — they are transcendental attributes of substance.
The measure account of unity is the ontological ground of the temporal identity condition. A substance is one (numerically one and the same) insofar as it has a single continuous measure across time. The same subject receives contraries through time while remaining one — Cat. Ch. 5 — because the measure of its identity (its form) persists through the change. The temporal identity condition of LNC is grounded in the unity of the substance as measure.
Ι.2 1053b24–1054a19
Is one a substance or an attribute? The Pythagorean and Platonic error
one as substance — fails
The error
The Pythagoreans and Plato treat one as a substance — the primary being from which all things derive. But if one were a substance, it would have to be predicated of itself — the One would have to be one. But then one is predicated of one, generating an infinite regress or self-predication.
The correction
One is not a substance but a transcendental attribute — said of every being insofar as it is a being, just as being is said of everything. One and being are convertible — everything that is, is one, and everything that is one, is. But neither is a substance; both are attributes of substance.
Original sources
Pythagoreans — the One as the primary substance; the Monad as the first principle of number and hence of all being
Plato — Republic 524d–526b — the One as the object of philosophical arithmetic; the One that the philosopher studies is not sensible but purely intelligible
Plato — Parmenides 137c–160b — the eight hypotheses about the One; Plato's own exhaustive examination of what follows if the One is or is not a substance
This resolves Beta Aporia 11 directly. One and being are transcendental attributes — said in as many ways as being is said, by reference to a primary sense. They are not substances; they are the most universal attributes of substance. This is why first philosophy investigates them — they belong to being qua being — but they do not add further entities to the ontology.
The error of treating one as a substance is structurally identical to Russell's error of treating sets as objects of the same type as their members. In both cases a transcendental attribute (one / membership) is treated as a first-order substance, and self-predication immediately generates paradox. The Aristotelian correction — one is a transcendental attribute, not a substance — is the ontological version of the type-distinction that prevents Russell's paradox.
Ι.3 1054a20–1054b14
Sameness, likeness, and equality — the relatives of unity
three kinds of unity of the many
The three relatives of unity
Sameness (ταὐτόν) — numerical unity across two descriptions: Socrates and the one sitting are the same. Sameness in substance and definition.
Likeness (ὅμοιον) — unity in quality: two white things are alike insofar as they have the same quality, though they are not numerically one.
Equality (ἴσον) — unity in quantity: two equal lines are alike insofar as they have the same measure, though they are numerically different.
The structure
Each relative of unity picks out a different category: sameness belongs to substance, likeness to quality, equality to quantity. This is the same categorical structure as the ten categories applied to the analysis of unity — the three relatives of one are one's expression in the three primary categories.
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 74a–c — the Equal Itself: the analysis of equality as a Form distinct from any pair of equal sensible things
Plato — Parmenides 139e–140b — the analysis of sameness and difference in relation to the One
Plato — Sophist 254d–259d — the five greatest kinds: being, rest, motion, same, different — same and different as the primary relatives of unity and plurality
The three relatives of unity map directly onto the first three elenchus conditions of De Int. Ch. 11: same subject (sameness in substance) · same predicate (sameness in definition) · same respect (sameness in quality or quantity). The elenchus conditions are the logical expression of the three relatives of unity — a genuine contradictory pair requires unity in all three.
The relatives of unity are the ontological ground of the elenchus conditions. To form a genuine contradictory pair requires sameness in substance (the subject is one), sameness in definition (the predicate is one), and sameness in respect (the quality or quantity being predicated is one). When any of these three unities is absent, the apparent contradiction is not genuine — it is a case of equivocation or amphiboly, which the Sophistical Refutations catalogues as fallacies.
Ι.4 1054b14–1055a33
Contrariety — the greatest difference: the primary structure of opposition
contrariety as the greatest difference within a genus — the key structure
The definition of contrariety
Contrariety is the greatest difference within a genus. Contraries are the extremes of a continuum — the most different things within the same genus. White and black are the most different within the genus of colour. Hot and cold are the most different within the genus of temperature. The contraries define the range of variation within their genus.
Why contrariety is the greatest difference
Contraries are the greatest difference because they have nothing intermediate that is more different from both than they are from each other. They are the limits of the genus — beyond the contraries there is nothing of that kind. This is why contraries define their genus: to know what colour is, is to know the range from white to black.
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 70e–71e — the argument from opposites: everything comes to be from its contrary; the living from the dead, the sleeping from the waking. Contrariety as the engine of becoming
Heraclitus — the unity of opposites: the way up and the way down are the same; day and night, summer and winter are one. Contrariety as the structure of the real
Anaximander — the hot and the cold, the wet and the dry as the primary contrary pairs from which all things arise
Plato — Sophist 257b–258e — difference and negation: the not-beautiful is not the contrary of the beautiful but merely different from it. Plato distinguishes contrariety from mere difference
Contrariety is the greatest difference within a genus — this is the precise definition that the elenchus conditions require. A genuine contradiction involves the same subject, same predicate, same respect — contraries cannot both be present simultaneously. But contraries are within the same genus; things in different genera do not even stand in the relation of contrariety. The conditions of LNC are grounded in the structure of contrariety.
Contrariety is the structure that makes LNC necessary and precise. Contraries cannot both be present in the same subject at the same time in the same respect — because they are the greatest difference within a genus, and the greatest difference within a genus cannot be simultaneously actualised in the same subject without destroying the subject's unity. The truth glut — the apparent simultaneous presence of contraries — is always a case of suppressed qualification: the contraries are present in different respects, at different times, or in different parts. Restoring the qualification shows that LNC is not violated.
Ι.5 1055a33–1056a34
Contrariety and privation — how privation is a form of contrariety
privation as one-sided contrariety
The argument
Privation (στέρησις) is a form of contrariety — specifically, the contrariety between the presence and the determined absence of a quality in a subject naturally capable of having it. Blindness is the privation of sight in a subject (an animal) that is naturally capable of sight. Privation is not mere absence (a stone does not have sight but is not blind) — it is the absence of what should be present.
The key distinction
Contrariety with intermediate: both contraries can be absent — a body need not be either white or black (it can be grey). Contrariety without intermediate (privation-possession pairs): one or the other must be present in the appropriate subject at the appropriate time — a mature animal either sees or is blind.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 257b–258e — the distinction between the not-F (mere difference from F) and the contrary of F (the greatest difference from F). Plato comes close to the privation analysis but does not fully develop it
Parmenides — non-being as the privation of being; the error of treating privation as absolute non-being rather than as a determined absence
Privation is the key to the Eleatic argument against change (Physics I.8). The Eleatics treat the privation of a quality as absolute non-being — and argue that change requires something to come from nothing. Aristotle's resolution: change requires privation (the determined absence of a form) not absolute non-being. The form comes from the privation — not from nothing.
This is the secundum quid dissolution of the Eleatic argument. The qualification suppressed: 'comes from non-being' should be 'comes from non-being-in-this-qualified-sense' — i.e., from privation. Restoring the qualification: change is from privation (the absence of a form in matter capable of receiving it) to the presence of the form. Not from absolute non-being. The Eleatic paradox dissolves.
Ι.6 1056a34–1057a17
Are contraries the same in genus? Can there be a contrary to substance?
contraries are always within the same genus — no contrary to substance
The argument
Contraries must be within the same genus — they are the greatest difference within a single domain. White and black are both colours; hot and cold are both temperatures; just and unjust are both qualities of the soul. There cannot be contraries across genera — a colour cannot be contrary to a number.
No contrary to primary substance
There is no contrary to any primary substance. What is the contrary of Socrates? Of this horse? Nothing. And there is no contrary to the species man or the genus animal. This is confirmed in Cat. Ch. 5: substance has no contrary. This is because the contraries define the range of variation within a genus — but substance is not a variation within a genus, it is the subject that the variations are about.
Original sources
Plato — Phaedo 102b–107b — the opposites analysis of change: the large becomes small, the hot becomes cold. Plato treats the Form as having a contrary (the Form of Large is contrary to the Form of Small)
Heraclitus — the unity of opposites implies contraries across all domains; Heraclitus does not restrict contrariety to within a genus
The restriction of contrariety to within a genus is a crucial logical principle: only things in the same genus can genuinely contradict each other. A predicate from one genus cannot genuinely conflict with a predicate from another genus — they simply apply to different aspects of the same subject. This grounds the elenchus condition 'same respect': a genuine contradiction requires both predicates to be in the same genus (the same respect).
This is the ontological ground of the 'same respect' condition of LNC. Apparent contradictions often suppress the genus-distinction — predications from different genera are treated as if they were in the same genus, generating apparent contradiction. Restoring the genus-distinction dissolves the apparent contradiction. Hot and cold cannot both be predicated of the same subject in the same respect — because they are contraries within the genus of temperature. But 'hot' (temperature) and 'square' (shape) can both be predicated of the same subject without contradiction — they are in different genera.
Ι.7 1057a18–1057b34
The intermediate — what is between the contraries?
the intermediate as participation in both contraries
The intermediate defined
Between contraries that admit of intermediates (white and black, hot and cold, good and bad) there are intermediate states — grey, warm, mediocre. The intermediate is constituted by participation in both contraries: grey is both somewhat white and somewhat black, in different degrees.
The structure of the intermediate
The intermediate is not a third thing added to the two contraries — it is what lies between them by participating in both. Change moves through the intermediates: from white through grey to black; from hot through warm to cold. The intermediates are the stages of the process of becoming.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 477a–c — the intermediate between knowledge and ignorance: opinion (δόξα) as what falls between being and non-being
Plato — Philebus 25e–26d — the mixed class: what participates in both the limit and the unlimited; the intermediate as the mixture of contraries
Plato — Timaeus 35a–b — the World Soul as the mixture of the same and the different, the indivisible and the divisible — the cosmic intermediate
The intermediate as participation in both contraries is the ontological structure of the superposition state. What is in the process of changing from white to black is both somewhat white and somewhat black — it genuinely participates in both contraries, in different degrees. This is not a violation of LNC — the contraries are present in different degrees (different respects) not simultaneously in the full sense.
The intermediate is the ontological form of the truth glut — not as a genuine contradiction but as a genuine superposition state. What is in the process of becoming is genuinely between the two contraries, participating in both to different degrees. This is the grounded presence of both predicates — dependent origination as incomplete actuality. Priest's dialethia is the logical description of the intermediate state; Aristotle's analysis of the intermediate shows that the state is real without being a genuine contradiction.
Ι.8 1057b35–1058b25
Difference and contrariety — what makes things different in species?
difference in species = contrariety of the differentia within the genus
The argument
Things are different in species when they are in the same genus but differ by a contrary differentia. Man and horse are different in species — both are animals (same genus) but they differ by contrary differentiae within the genus (two-footed vs four-footed, rational vs non-rational). Species difference is contrariety of the differentia within the genus.
The key implication
Things in different genera are not different in species — they are simply other. A colour and a number are not different species of the same genus; they are in entirely different genera. Species difference requires a shared genus — you can only be differently specifically from something you share a genus with.
Original sources
Plato — Sophist 257c–258c — difference and negation: the not-beautiful is not the contrary of the beautiful — it includes everything that is not beautiful, however different from beauty. Plato distinguishes mere difference from contrariety
Plato — Statesman 262a–263e — the error of dividing by mere difference (Greeks vs barbarians) rather than by genuine differentia; the demand for natural division by contraries within the genus
Species difference as contrariety of the differentia within the genus confirms the biological taxonomy. The differentiae that divide a genus must be contraries within the genus — the correct biological differentiae (blooded/bloodless, viviparous/oviparous) are genuine contraries, not mere differences. This connects Iota.8 directly to PA I.2–3 and HA I.1 — the method of biological classification is grounded in the ontology of contrariety.
The connection between species difference and contrariety of the differentia is the ontological ground of the Porphyrian tree as a truth-condition structure. The differentiae that divide genera into species are contraries — they are the greatest differences within the genus. This means the per se attributes of a species are those that follow from the species' position on the tree — from the chain of contrary differentiae that define it. The truth conditions are grounded in contrariety all the way down.
Ι.9 1058b26–1059a14
Why are male and female not different species of animal?
contrary differentiae within the same matter — not species-level contrariety
The aporia
Male and female are contrary differentiae of animal — they are the greatest difference within the genus with respect to reproduction. By the principle of Iota.8, contraries of the differentia within a genus generate species difference. But male and female are not different species — a male man and a female man are both men.
The resolution
Male and female are contrary differentiae that belong to the matter, not to the form. The form of man is the same in male and female — both are rational animals with the same essential nature. The male-female difference affects the material organisation (the reproductive matter) but not the formal essence. Species difference requires contrary differentiae of the form; male-female difference is contrary differentiation of the matter.
Original sources
Plato — Republic 454d–e — Socrates argues that male and female differ only in the capacity to beget and bear children — in all other respects relevant to virtue and political capacity, they are the same. The difference is material, not formal
Plato — Timaeus 90e–91d — the female as a later creation for the purpose of reproduction; the material difference grounded in the cosmic plan
The resolution connects to GA I.1 — the male provides the formal-efficient cause; the female provides the material cause. The form is the same in both; the material role in generation differs. This is the metaphysical ground of the biological analysis of sexual difference.
The distinction between formal and material contrariety is a precise application of the secundum quid move. Male and female are contrary — but the contrariety is in the matter (the reproductive function), not in the form (the essence of the species). Treating the material contrariety as if it were formal contrariety would make male and female different species — a suppression of the form/matter qualification. Restoring the qualification: the contrariety is in the matter secundum quid, not in the form simpliciter.
Ι.10 1058b26–1059a14
Why are perishable and imperishable things not different species of the same genus?
perishable / imperishable = difference in form, not merely in matter
The argument
The perishable and the imperishable differ more than things that differ in species. A perishable man and an imperishable god are not different species of the same genus (animal) — they are more different than that. Perishability and imperishability are not merely contrary differentiae of the same form — they affect the form itself. A perishable being is constituted differently at the most fundamental level from an imperishable one.
Original sources
Plato — Timaeus 41a–d — the gods (immortal) and mortals (perishable) created differently; the distinction between the imperishable and the perishable runs deeper than species difference
Plato — Phaedo 78c–80c — the Forms are imperishable; the sensible things that participate in them are perishable; the imperishable and the perishable belong to entirely different ontological levels
This resolves Beta Aporia 10 — perishable and imperishable things do not have the same principles in exactly the same sense. The imperishable substance (the Unmoved Mover of Lambda) has form without matter — pure actuality. The perishable substance has form in matter — compound actuality. The difference is not merely in the duration of existence but in the ontological structure.
Perishability and imperishability as formal (not merely material) differences is the ontological ground of the apophatic limit. The Unmoved Mover is not merely a substance that happens not to perish — it is a substance of an entirely different ontological type: pure actuality without matter, pure form without potentiality. This is why Lambda.9 falls silent: the Unmoved Mover is not a member of the same genus as perishable substances, and language — which describes things within genera by their differentiae — cannot reach it.
The movement of Iota — the ontological structure of unity and contrariety:

Unity: One is a transcendental attribute — the measure of each kind of being. Not a substance (Iota.2). Its three relatives — sameness, likeness, equality — are the ontological ground of the first three elenchus conditions of De Int. Ch. 11.

Contrariety: The greatest difference within a genus. Contraries define the range of variation within their genus. No contrary to substance. The intermediate between contraries participates in both — the ontological form of the superposition state.

Species difference: Contrariety of the differentia within the genus — the ontological ground of the Porphyrian tree as a truth-condition structure.

The limits of contrariety: Male-female difference is material, not formal (Iota.9). Perishable-imperishable difference exceeds species difference — it is a difference in ontological type (Iota.10). At the limit: the Unmoved Mover is not a contrary of anything — it is outside all contrariety, pure actuality, beyond the structure of genus and differentia. This is Lambda.9's silence.
Metaphysics Iota is the logical complement to Zeta. Where Zeta asked what primary substance is (form), Iota asks what makes a substance one (the measure), what are the relations of unity (sameness, likeness, equality), and what is the structure of the opposition that governs change within a genus (contrariety). Together Zeta and Iota give the complete account of substance: what it is (form) and what makes it one (unity through form) and how it changes while remaining one (through the contraries, via the intermediates, while the form persists). The elenchus conditions of De Int. Ch. 11 are grounded throughout in the ontological analysis of Iota: same subject (Iota.1 — numerical unity), same predicate (Iota.3 — sameness in definition), same respect (Iota.6 — within the same genus), same time (Iota.4 — contraries cannot be simultaneously present in the primary sense). Iota is the ontological foundation of the logical apparatus of the entire project. March 2026.
